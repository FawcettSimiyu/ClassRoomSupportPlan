Classroom Support Plan
Comprehensive guide for elementary teachers (grade 2)

Part 1 · Introduction

We are learners, leaders, and achievers. We believe in ourselves, our peers, and our community. We will change the world!

mission in action This promise, adapted from school improvement frameworks, is posted daily and referenced during morning meeting. It reminds every student that our classroom is a community of growth (Wong & Wong, 2018).

Class Demographics · 22 Second‑Graders

First‑year takeaway: Create a “class family” bulletin board with photos and family‑drawn flags to celebrate backgrounds from day one.

Family Engagement Strategies

Weekly 'Learning Journey' – digital + paper newsletter: key concepts, vocabulary, and “ask me about…” prompts (Epstein, 2011). Includes a photo of the week.
Coffee with the Class – quarterly informal morning meetings (virtual/in‑person) where families observe morning meeting and ask questions.
Family Expert visits – invite family to share cultural traditions, careers, or hobbies; ties to curriculum (e.g., community helpers).
Two‑way communication – Friday updates, digital portfolios (Seesaw), open observation slots; quick positive postcards sent home.
Family reading nights – twice per year, families come for storytelling and literacy games; children showcase work.
Volunteer classroom helpers – sign‑up for reading partners, prep materials, or sharing a skill; training provided.

All communications are translated via school translation services; use a communication log to track outreach.

Part 2 · Structured Teaching Environment

Classroom Layout: The Third Teacher

🧩 whole‑group carpet (morning meeting, mini‑lesson)
🗂️ small‑group tables (x3) – collaboration
🛋️ quiet nook / beanbags (self‑regulation)
📚 library & listening (diverse books)
🧺 materials shelves (labeled with words + pictures)
💺 flexible seating: wobble stools, cushions
👩‍🏫 teacher table (small‑group intervention)
🔲 standing/clipboard area for independent work

Why it works: Zones support collaboration, calm, and independence. Students choose where to work during “choice time” (Strong‑Wiles & Hermon, 2020).

Matrix is taught through role‑play, daily reviews for first month, then weekly (Horner et al., 2010).

Classroom Rules (Co‑Created with Students)

✨ Be respectful ✨ Be responsible ✨ Be an upstander ✨ Always do your personal best

During first week, students share what each rule looks like/sounds like. We sign a class contract (Kriete, 2014).

Teaching Methods

  • Explicit instruction (I do, we do, you do) – Lemov (2015). Used for new content and procedures.
  • Hands‑on / experiential – science experiments, math manipulatives, project‑based learning.
  • Student choice – choice boards, “must do / may do” during literacy centers.
  • Collaborative learning – partner talk, group investigations, think‑pair‑share.
  • Behavior‑specific praise – “I noticed how Maria pushed in her chair responsibly.” Reinforces matrix.

Part 3 · Routines & Procedures

First‑year cornerstone: All routines are taught using the 5‑step process: introduce, discuss, model, practice, review (Wong & Wong, 2018). Each lesson plan includes teacher language and practical tips. Click any routine to expand.
1. Morning Arrival & Getting Started
  • 1 Introduce (day 1): “A calm morning helps us learn. Let’s learn our arrival steps.”
  • 2 Discuss: “What do we need to do first? (hang coat, unpack folder, put lunch away).” List on chart.
  • 3 Model: Teacher acts as student: enters, hangs backpack, places folder in bin, starts morning warm‑up. Also show incorrect (wandering, loud).
  • 4 Practice: 3‑4 students at a time role‑play while class gives thumbs up/down. Repeat until all can do it.
  • 5 Review: For first two weeks, review steps each morning. Use specific praise: “Zara, you remembered to put your folder in the bin immediately!”
Teacher language: “When you come in, first hang your bag, then put your folder in the bin, then start your morning puzzle. Watch me.”
Personal tip: Post a visual checklist near the door with pictures for each step.
2. Lining Up & Hallway Travel
  • 1 Introduce: “We are leaders in the hallway – we keep everyone safe and quiet.”
  • 2 Discuss: “What does ‘Be Safe’ look like? (hands to self, face forward). What about ‘Be Kind’? (silent so others can learn).”
  • 3 Model: Signal: “Spartan Stars, line up.” Teacher raises hand, students freeze and raise hand, then call tables. Show silent line, bubble space.
  • 4 Practice: Walk to specials (art, music). If talking or wiggling, calmly return and repeat. “We’ll try again until it’s safe.”
  • 5 Review: Before every transition first month. Use a “hallway hero” award.

Personal note: Use a “magic bubble” wand to remind kids to keep personal space. They love it.

3. Getting Help (Ask 3 Before Me)
  • 1 Introduce: “We are problem‑solvers. Before you ask me, try three things.”
  • 2 Discuss: Anchor chart: 1) Look at the board/anchor chart, 2) Ask a partner quietly, 3) Try another part, then raise hand.
  • 3 Model: Student role‑play: gets stuck, looks at chart, then asks partner, then raises hand while continuing work.
  • 4 Practice: During independent work, teacher intentionally delays response. Praise those who try strategies.
  • 5 Review: Role‑play weekly for first month; refer to anchor chart daily.
4. End‑of‑Day Dismissal
  • 1 Introduce: “A calm dismissal means everyone gets home safely and we don’t forget things.”
  • 2 Discuss: Steps: clean workspace, pack backpack, put folder in bag, quiet reading at desk, wait for group call.
  • 3 Model: Teacher shows cleaning, packing, then reading silently.
  • 4 Practice: 15‑minute countdown with timer. Praise tables that are ready. Dismiss by groups (bus, car, walk).
  • 5 Review: After any messy day, debrief next morning. Re‑teach after breaks.

Example language: “Table 2 is completely ready – they may line up for their bus group.”

5. Fire Drill (Emergency)
  • 1 Introduce: “Fire drills keep us safe. We must be silent and fast.”
  • 2 Discuss: Signal: loud alarm. Stop, line up silently at door, teacher leads out to blacktop. Sit and wait for attendance.
  • 3 Model: Walk the route to blacktop, show where to sit. Practice “freeze and line” inside.
  • 4 Practice: First drill is announced; afterward, debrief what went well.
  • 5 Review: Before each monthly drill, remind expectations. Use a calm voice – avoid scaring.

From experience: After a drill, always say, “You kept us safe by being silent. I’m proud.”

Pro tip: After long breaks, re‑teach one routine per day using the same 5‑step process.
